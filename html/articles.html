How to prepare for a long walk

A long walk is a mini-journey that can offer incredible sights, reveal new corners of the city, and boost your energy. But to ensure your enjoyment isn't spoiled by fatigue or unexpected hassles, a little preparation goes a long way. Just a few simple steps can turn your outing into the perfect adventure.

1. The Foundation of Comfort

The important rule: shoes should be broken-in, not brand new. Opt for sneakers or walking shoes with good cushioning and arch support. Save the stiff leather boots or thin canvas shoes for shorter strolls.

Dress for the weather, in layers. Even on a warm day, a light windbreaker in your backpack is a lifesaver. Choose breathable, moisture-wicking fabrics (think performance materials, fleece).

2. What to Bring

Don't overpack. Bring only the necessities:

  • Water. A small 0.5L bottle is the minimum. For walks over 3 hours, aim for 1-1.5L.
  • A Light Snack. Energy bars, nuts, fruit, or chocolate. They’ll save you from a sudden energy drop.
  • Hand Sanitizer & Wet Wipes. For on-the-go hygiene.
  • Portable Charger (Power Bank). Keep your maps, camera, and music going.
  • Mini First-Aid Kit. Band-aids (blister-specific ones are great!) and pain relievers.
  • Sunscreen & a Hat (for sunny days).

3. Planning Your Route

A good route is more than just a line on a map.

Pick key points of interest (landmarks, cafes, parks), but be realistic about distances. It’s better to explore one neighborhood deeply than to rush across the entire city.

Plan your start and end points (nearest subway, bus stops). Where can you bail out if you get tired early?

Factor in time not just for walking, but for photos, coffee breaks, and simply taking it all in. The perfect pace is when you’re not constantly checking the time.

Use your app to note the location of public restrooms and good spots to eat within your budget.

4. The Right Mindset

Bring headphones for a playlist or audio guide, but remember to take them out sometimes and listen to the city around you. Be open to small detours—the most memorable discoveries often happen off the planned path.

Photo walk ideas: How to take unique photos

A photo walk is exploring the city and being creative. Whether you use a DSLR or a smartphone, unique shots come from your perspective, not just your gear. Let's refine that perspective.

1. Start with a Theme, Then Plan the Route

Don't just go out to "take some pics." Choose a theme—it focuses your eye and creates a cohesive story.

  • Urban Geometry: Look for lines, patterns, and symmetry in architecture, staircases, windows, railings.
  • Life in Details: A series on door handles, street lamps, signs, graffiti, puddle reflections.
  • Quiet Corners: Seek out the most secluded, atmospheric courtyards or small squares.
  • People in the City: Capture silhouettes, reflections, hands, feet—without making a face the main subject.

2. Play with Light

The "Golden Hour" - the hour after sunrise and before sunset. Soft, warm, directional light transforms ordinary scenes.

The "Blue Hour" - twilight, just after sunset - bathes the city in deep blue while lights turn on. Perfect for long exposures to capture light trails.

Harsh midday sun isn't a bad time. Look for dramatic shadows, shoot under arches, or use tree leaves as a natural filter.

3. Angle is important

Stop shooting everything from eye level. This is the #1 tip for unique shots.

1. Get Low. Shoot flowers, buildings, people looking up. It adds drama and scale.

2. Find a High Vantage Point. A bridge, stairs, or a hill reveals patterns and connections in the cityscape.

3. Look for Reflections. Puddles, shop windows, glass facades, mirrors—they create surreal and memorable frames.

4. Use Natural Framing. Shoot through an archway, between tree branches, or from a window. It creates a "picture within a picture" and guides the viewer's eye.

4. Locations to Add to Your Route

  • Industrial Areas & Backstreets. Full of gritty textures and striking graffiti.
  • Central Markets. A burst of color, texture, and life.
  • Waterfronts. Canals, embankments, and fountains offer great symmetry and reflection shots.
  • Staircases & Bridges. They are powerful leading lines themselves.

5. Post-Processing

Don't skip a little editing. Use mobile apps to fine-tune the mood, adjust exposure, or tweak the color tone to match your theme.

The art of the winter city walk

A winter walk through the city requires a special approach. With the right preparation, your outing can be comfortable, safe, and incredibly picturesque.

1. Gear Up: The Layering Principle

The main enemy of a winter walk isn't the cold—it's sweat, which cools rapidly in the freezing air and leads to hypothermia.

The three-layer system is ideal:
1) Base Layer (thermal underwear) – wicks moisture.
2) Mid Layer (fleece, wool) – insulates and retains heat.
3) Outer Layer (waterproof/windproof shell) – protects from the elements.

Critical Item: Waterproof boots with a thick, insulated sole and good tread. The air gap in the sole provides warmth, and the grip prevents slips.

Don't forget the accessories: Warm, windproof mittens (warmer than gloves), a hat, and a neck gaiter or scarf to protect your face in the wind.

2. Planning Your Route

In winter, it's important to think about practicality as much as beauty.

Shorter & Richer. Daylight is scarce, and moving through snow or ice is slower. Plan a more compact route than you would in summer, but pack it with points of interest.

Warm-up Stops. Build regular "warming points" into your route: a museum with a cozy café, a bookstore, an indoor market, a mall.

Safety First. Avoid unlit, potentially icy park paths. Opt for central, well-maintained streets and scenic embankments with beautiful views.

3. What to Pack

A winter explorer's backpack has some special items:

1. A thermos with hot tea, coffee, or cocoa. Nothing warms you from the inside better and creates a special feeling of coziness.

2. A compact sit pad (like foam) – for sitting on a bench without getting cold.

3. Power banks lose charge faster in the cold, so keep yours in an inner jacket pocket.

4. Extra socks and gloves. Just in case your feet get wet or you lose a mitten.

4. Winter Aesthetics

Winter transforms the urban landscape, offering new perspectives for contemplation and photography.

The best time for a walk is a clear day after a snowfall. Everything is white, pristine, and the sun makes the snow sparkle.

Evening illuminations reflected in snow and ice also create a beautiful atmosphere. Plan part of your route for twilight.

How to Save Time and Not Miss Anything

Visiting a new city for a few days or just want to make the most of a weekend at home? The feeling that you need to see everything, but time is critically short, is familiar to everyone. The key to success isn't running faster; it's smarter planning. Here’s how to see the maximum without turning your trip into a marathon.

1. Prioritization

Make a "wish list" of everything you'd like to see. Now, divide it into three categories:

  • Must to see: The 2-3 iconic landmarks you're essentially there for.
  • Want to see: Interesting second-tier museums, districts, parks.
  • Nice to see: Quaint cafes, niche shops, distant viewpoints.

The 80/20 rule applies: 20% of the locations will give you 80% of the impressions. Focus on those.

2. Geo-Clustering

Group attractions by neighborhood. Dedicate one day or half-day to deeply exploring a single historic district. You'll save hours on transit and actually absorb the local atmosphere.

Plan your flow. Start your route at one subway station and end at another, moving in a logical trajectory. Avoid backtracking.

3. Time-Saving Hacks

Pre-book e-tickets for popular museums. This can save you 30 minutes to 2 hours of queueing.

Don't block out a separate two-hour restaurant lunch. Plan for a street food snack (crepes, pies, sandwiches) from a reputable spot or a coffee-and-pastry break at a local bakery. It saves time and immerses you in the culture.

Download the city map in Google Maps or Maps.me for offline use. Many museums offer free audio guides via their app—listen while you're in line or walking between sights.

4. Balanced Pacing

Don't rush yourself. Between must-see stops, leave room for the unplanned.

Build in buffer time. After each major sight, leave a 15-20 minute "cushion" for a suddenly captivating view, a chat with a local, or an taking photos.

Listen to yourself. If you're museum-ed out, feel free to skip the next one on the list and head to a park instead. Your genuine enjoyment is more important than checking a box.

End on a high. It's better to finish the day earlier, feeling pleasantly tired with vivid memories, than exhausted from having seen everything on a list.

Budget travel: How to avoid spending too much

Traveling doesn't have to be expensive. A little strategy and a focus on authentic experiences will allow you to deeply explore new cities without breaking the bank.

1. Rethink your excursions.

Instead of buying tickets to every major museum, prioritize the city's free attractions.

Public parks, historic neighborhoods, architectural walks, and bustling street markets often provide the most complete experience of a place.

Many museums also offer free days or hours—a quick online search before your trip can save you a ton of money.

2. Eat and drink like a local.

Avoid tourist trap restaurants in the main square and downtown. Look for places where locals line up for lunch, visit bakeries for morning pastries, and explore food markets for fresh, affordable produce and snacks.

Cafes and eateries catering to locals don't usually have huge advertising banners or large, brightly decorated entrances. They can also be located in courtyards and be completely unnoticeable.

3. Walk.

This is a good way to save money on taxis if the distances aren't too long. By walking, you also explore the city and stumble upon hidden corners that you'd never see from a bus or taxi.

You control your own pace and can stop whenever something catches your eye.

4. Choose reasonable accommodation.

Consider alternatives to hotels in the city center. A room in a residential area rented through a platform can be cheaper and offer a more authentic atmosphere.

There are also hostels, which are even more affordable. There you can chat with your roommates and learn new things about the city and ask for helpful tips.

Look for places with good public transportation links to the city center or, even better, within walking distance of attractions.

5. Chat with others.

You can find a community or group on social media for the city you're interested in.

  • There, you can find locals and ask them for tips on saving on accommodation, transportation, and food.
  • Meet other travelers who want to visit the city. You can not only have a great time but also save on accommodation by renting a shared apartment or splitting taxi fares.

The goal of budget travel is not to miss out on anything important, but to be more mindful. It's about trading expensive entrance fees for deeper immersion, gourmet meals for authentic flavors, and passive excursions for active exploration.

Tips for walking if you're walking alone

Solo walks are a wonderful experience, a chance to move at your own pace, set your own schedule, and not have to fit in with other people.

1. Useful Skill

By constantly looking for or waiting for people to spend time with you, you can miss out on a lot. Learn to comfortably spend time alone, and such walks will become a wonderful pastime.

You'll learn new things not only about the city but also about yourself, because when you're alone with your thoughts, you'll have no choice but to talk to yourself and ask new questions, understanding yourself better and better.

2. Your Own Pace

Everyone is different, and everyone needs their own time for everything: to eat, to look at a painting, to walk across a bridge, and to pet a cat.

When walking alone, you choose the time for each process—you don't make anyone wait for you, or you don't wait for anyone.

There are places you'd like to go to, and none of your friends or acquaintances would, so going there alone is the only right choice.

3. Change of plans

Sometimes your mood changes at the last minute and you want to completely change or even cancel an outing, but if you've already made plans with someone, it would be impolite to let the person who planned to spend time with you down. To avoid ruining your relationship with that person, you have to go for a walk.

If you're walking alone, you can change your plans as much as you like, however convenient.

4. Missing out on moments

Being distracted by conversations can lead you to miss beautiful views, interesting outfits of passersby, the sounds of the forest or river, and much more.

Listening to the noise of the city helps you understand it better and become more immersed in it, not missing any details. Enjoy the silence and the views.

For added comfort, consider bringing a camera or a journal to record your impressions of what you see. Plan your perfect personal journey and enjoy connecting with your thoughts and the world around you.
